Systems Engineering Task 1

This is the first task required of me to complete in systems engineering 2017 VCE unit 1.

Design Brief

In this project I was required to design and create a windpowered vehicle. It was to be powered by the force of wind as the final source of movement. There were no batteries allowed to be used at all in the product. It was to have a maximum width of 200mm and length of 300mm. I also had to hand in this folio of everything I did and my thought process.

Main Idea

I plan to create a thin and light plywood base. Attach a straw to the ends of the plywood and fit a skewer in the straw; and poke skewer ends into bottle caps to be used as wheels. Possibly craft a small ramp so the balloon doesn’t make contact with the wheels. Tape a straw around the inside of the mouth piece of the balloon. Attach straw facing backwards for forward momentum.

Alternative Idea

Another Idea I have is to use a water bottle (600ml) to replace the plywood from previous idea. The pros of doing this is that I will not have to risk getting the wrong measurements on the plywood and it will be a lot lighter. The cons is that it might not have enough weight to give a straight forward momentum, hypothetically. Everything else would remain the same.

Materials & Uses

• Plywood, I used plywood because it is light, structurally strong and was something easily accessible to me through school supplies.
• Straws, the straws are going to be used as a chamber to hold skewers which will turn inside of the straw loosely providing little resistance between the two. One will also be used to create pressurised air for the balloon.
• Skewers, these would be attached to the wheels and rotates inside of the straw being held in one spot loosely so it can turn freely.
• Balloon, the balloon is going to be used to hold the source of wind that is required for my wind powered vehicle to work.
• Duct tape, this will hold down the wheel axels and the straw attached to the balloon.
• Bottle caps, I used bottle caps to be used as wheels when attached to the skewers and rotate when the pressure from the balloon is released.

Material Sustainability

Plywood –

FSC: The Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) is the only certification scheme on the market that guarantees timber products that come from legal and responsibly managed sources. Wood labelled by the FSC comes from forests or plantations that are well managed according to strict environmentally and socially responsible standards.
OSB: Oriented Strand Board (OSB) is a composite wood product made from layers of wood bonded with wax and resin adhesives. FSC certified OSB is a greener alternative to tropical plywood and is priced competitively. It is no different from plywood in terms of its quality and workability.
FSC certified plywood: Plywood that is certified by the FSC indicates that it is made from sustainable sources and does not come from endangered tropical rainforests or illegal sources. It can be used in places where OSB is not available or practical

Soft Plywood

Softwood panel is usually made either of cedar, Douglas fir or spruce, pine, and fir (collectively known as spruce-pine-fir or SPF) or redwood and is typically used for construction and industrial purposes. The most common dimension is 1.2 by 2.4 metres (3 ft 11 in × 7 ft 10 in) or the slightly larger imperial dimension of 4 feet × 8 feet. Plies vary in thickness from 1.4 mm to 4.3 mm. The number of plies—which is always odd—depends on the thickness and grade of the sheet. Roofing can use the thinner 5/8" (15 mm) plywood. Subfloors are at least 3/4" (18 mm) thick, the thickness depending on the distance between floor joists. Plywood for flooring applications is often tongue and groove; this prevents one board from moving up or down relative to its neighbour, so providing a solid-feeling floor when the joints do not lie over joists. T&G plywood is usually found in the 1/2" to 1" (12–25 mm) range.

Hard Plywood

Hardwood plywood is made out of wood from angiosperm trees and used for demanding end uses. Hardwood plywood is characterized by its excellent strength, stiffness and resistance to creep. It has a high planar shear strength and impact resistance, which make it especially suitable for heavy-duty floor and wall structures. Oriented plywood construction has a high wheel-carrying capacity. Hardwood plywood has excellent surface hardness, and damage- and wear-resistance.

Week 1

Begin research of various different ideas for our wind powered vehicle. I went onto the internet and began researching different ways wind can power something in a kinetic way. I learned that I could use a clockwork design but it was a lot of work and would be heavy probably ending up in not enough work to even begin moving the vehicle because of the weight from the clockwork design. I looked into a way to ‘kick-start’ a small electrical motor using kinetic energy and have the motor power a propeller on the back to create motion of wind and move it forwards. I also did not like this design idea because it was difficult to work a way to “kick-start” an electric motor without actual electricity. I found later that day after looking through various different designs that I could use a balloon to store the wind and pressurise it on its way out creating a large enough force to push the vehicle forwards with only the use of wind.
